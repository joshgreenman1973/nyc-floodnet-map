Where the streets flood

A growing network of city sensors is, for the first time, measuring exactly when and how deep New York City streets flood — block by block, in real time. This maps every reading.

FloodNet is a research partnership between the City University of New York, New York University and New York City agencies that places ultrasonic depth sensors at chronic flooding hot spots. Each dot below is one sensor; its size and color reflect how often it has recorded flooding. Coastal sensors driven by tides are shown separately from those triggered by rainfall.

Methodology, sources and limitations

Data sources. Two datasets published on NYC Open Data: FloodNet: Street Flooding Events Measured by FloodNet Sensors (aq7i-eu5q) and FloodNet: Sensor Deployment Metadata (kb2e-tjy3). FloodNet is a collaboration of CUNY, NYU and city agencies. Data pulled .

What is counted. A "flood event" is a continuous period in which a sensor measured standing water above its detection threshold. Events are joined to sensors on sensor_id to attach borough, location and the tidal-influence flag. "Peak depth" is the maximum depth reached during an event; "duration" is the published event length in minutes.

The network-growth caveat (read this). Rising yearly event totals largely reflect a rapidly expanding sensor network, not necessarily more flooding. The network grew from a handful of sensors in 2020 to today. The "per 100 active sensor-months" view normalizes by exposure: for each year we count how many sensor-months were live (a sensor is treated as active from its install date forward) and divide events by that. This is the more honest trend line, though it still cannot fully separate weather variation from placement choices — sensors are deliberately sited at known hot spots, so these numbers describe flooding where the city expects it, not a random sample of streets. Confidence: high for raw counts and depths; medium for the normalized series.

Tidal vs rainfall. The split uses FloodNet's own tidally_influenced flag on each sensor. Tidal sensors sit in coastal, often daily-flooding locations; their high event counts reflect routine tidal inundation, not storms.

Limitations. Sensors cover only a fraction of city streets and are concentrated in flood-prone, environmental-justice-priority areas. Absence of a sensor is not evidence of no flooding. A small number of events or sensors may lack complete metadata and are omitted from the affected chart only. Nothing here is modeled or estimated — every value is a published measurement.

How this was made. The data pipeline, map and charts were built with AI assistance (Claude). Every number is computed directly from the two FloodNet datasets above by a transparent script — no figures are AI-estimated or invented. Checks performed: row counts reconciled against the source totals; the latitude/longitude orientation and sensor join were validated against sample records; the normalized series was hand-verified for two years. Not independently checked: FloodNet's own sensor calibration and event-detection thresholds, which we take as published. If you spot something off, treat the linked source datasets as ground truth.
