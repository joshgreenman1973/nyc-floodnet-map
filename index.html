Severity, not just frequency

Most floods are puddles. A few are dangerous.

Counting events alone treats a film of curb water the same as water over a car's hood. Every measured flood here is graded by how deep it actually got, and by how many hours the water stood above a foot, the point where a street turns impassable.

Method, sources and what this cannot tell you

Sources. Two NYC Open Data sets published by FloodNet, the research collaboration of CUNY, NYU and city agencies that builds and runs the sensors: flood events (aq7i-eu5q) and sensor deployment metadata (kb2e-tjy3). Everything on this page is computed by build_data.py straight from those two files. Nothing is modelled, estimated or smoothed. The record runs from .

Why the stable-corner section exists. The network grew from 2 sensors to , so a citywide yearly total mostly tracks deployment rather than weather. The fix is to hold the sensor set fixed. That is the only comparison on this page that attempts a like-for-like reading across years.

Survivorship was checked, and it mattered. An earlier version of this page divided by all 34 sensors installed before 2023. Eleven of those have since been retired, and a sensor that is gone records no floods, so leaving it in the denominator manufactures a decline. The cohort here is the sensors that were in the ground before 2023 and are still in FloodNet's live deployment list, which raises the per-corner figures by roughly 40 percent against the flawed version. This is a proxy for uptime, not proof of it: a surviving sensor could still have been offline for stretches, and the published event record cannot show that.

What the stable-corner numbers do and do not support. They are a real measurement of what those corners experienced. They are not a citywide climate trend, for three reasons stated plainly. The sample is small, and in any given year only of the corners recorded a flood at all, so a handful of chronic spots move the totals. Three years is weather, not climate. And these particular corners were chosen early precisely because they were known trouble spots, often right after a bad flood, so some regression toward calmer years is expected regardless of the weather. The two measures also disagree about which year was worst, which is itself the useful finding.

Placement is not random, anywhere on this page. FloodNet puts sensors where flooding is expected. A corner with a big circle is a place the city chose to watch and where water duly arrived. A block with no sensor is not a dry block, it is an unmeasured one. Absence of a dot is never evidence of absence of flooding.

Definitions. A flood event is FloodNet's own detection, with an observed floor of 0.43 inches held for 5 minutes. Depth is the peak of the event. Deep-water hours count time above 12 inches. Tidal versus rainfall follows the tidally_influenced flag on the sensor, so it describes the driver at that location rather than the cause of a particular storm.

Confidence. High for every raw measurement: depth, duration, location, counts. Medium for the stable-corner comparison, which depends on the survivorship proxy above and on a small sample. This page makes no claim about a citywide trend in flooding, and the live companion page makes none either.
