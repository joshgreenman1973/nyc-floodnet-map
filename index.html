Methodology, sources & the honest-trend math

Sources. Two NYC Open Data datasets: FloodNet Street Flooding Events (aq7i-eu5q) and FloodNet Sensor Deployment Metadata (kb2e-tjy3). FloodNet is a CUNY / NYU / city-agency collaboration. Pulled ; events span .

Severity. Each event is graded by peak depth: Nuisance (<4″), Notable (4–8″), Serious (8–12″), Severe (12–24″), Extreme (24″+). Deep-flood-hours sum the published duration_above_12_inches_mins across events — the time water stood above a foot. This weights a long, deep flood far above a brief puddle.

Why raw counts mislead — and how we fix it. The sensor network grew roughly 30× over the period (2 sensors in late 2020 to now), so raw yearly event totals mostly track deployment, not weather. Two corrections: per active sensor divides each year's events by the sensors live that year (annualized for partial years); and the stable cohort — the sensors already live before 2023 — is tracked alone across 2023–2025, holding the sensor set fixed. Within that fixed set, events per sensor fell over 2023–2025, the opposite of the raw trend. Confidence: high for raw measurements; medium for the per-sensor and cohort series (small early cohort; year-to-year weather varies; sensors sit at known hot spots, not a random street sample).

Partial years. 2020 is six weeks (network began Nov 16, 2 events) and 2026 runs through early June. Both are flagged and excluded from cohort comparisons.

How this was made. The pipeline, map and charts were built with AI assistance (Claude). Every value is computed directly from the two FloodNet datasets by a transparent script — nothing is AI-estimated. Checks: event and sensor counts reconciled with source totals; the per-sensor and stable-cohort math hand-verified for 2023–2025; the lat/lon orientation and event→sensor join validated against sample records. Not independently checked: FloodNet's own sensor calibration and detection thresholds, taken as published. Treat the linked datasets as ground truth.
