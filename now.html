Method, sources and honesty notes

Source. All readings come live from the FloodNet public API (api.floodnet.nyc), the research collaboration of CUNY, NYU and New York City agencies that builds and maintains the sensors. Sensor locations come from GET /api/rest/deployments/flood; depth readings from the network's public GraphQL endpoint, queried for the trailing 24 hours and refreshed every 60 seconds. Depths shown are FloodNet's processed measurement (depth_proc_mm), converted to inches. Nothing is modeled, interpolated or estimated by this page.

What "wet" means. A sensor counts as wet when its most recent processed reading in the last 15 minutes is greater than zero. FloodNet's processing already filters sensor noise; shallow readings under about an inch are typically puddling, ponding or tidal film rather than dangerous flooding. The depth ladder used for color: under 4″ nuisance · 4–8″ notable · 8–12″ serious · 12–24″ severe · over 24″ extreme.

What this page cannot tell you. Sensors sit where FloodNet chose to put them — biased toward known flood-prone blocks. A dry map means the sensors are dry, not that no street in the city has water on it. Some sensors go offline (shown hollow); their silence is not evidence of dry pavement. The API is an early-access beta and field definitions can change.

Confidence. High for the readings themselves (they are FloodNet's own published measurements, fetched unmodified). The 15-minute "wet right now" window and the 24-hour aggregations are this page's choices; both are stated above and visible in the page source.
