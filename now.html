Method, sources and what this cannot tell you

Live source. Readings come from the FloodNet public API (api.floodnet.nyc), the research collaboration of CUNY, NYU and New York City agencies that builds and runs the sensors. Sensor locations from GET /api/rest/deployments/flood; depths from the network's GraphQL endpoint. The live state refreshes every 60 seconds and the 24-hour history every five minutes, which keeps the load on a research API reasonable. Depths are FloodNet's processed measurement (depth_proc_mm) converted to inches. Nothing is modeled or estimated here.

The comparison problem, and how it is handled. The live API streams raw per-minute depths. The historical record on NYC Open Data (aq7i-eu5q) contains only detected flood events. Counting every reading above zero today and setting it against detected events would compare a loose standard to a strict one and manufacture a spike out of nothing. So build_history.py reads the effective floor out of FloodNet's own published events, which is 0.43 inches held for 5 minutes, and this page applies that same floor to live readings. Shallower water is shown on the map and labeled puddling, but it is never counted in any comparison.

Depth means water that stood, not the highest sample. The figure on each gauge is the deepest level held across at least five consecutive readings within a 15-minute span, which matches the five-minute duration floor FloodNet applies to its own published events. This matters because some sensors emit a lone spike. One reported a single reading of 28.9 inches while sitting at 1 to 2 inches for the nineteen hours either side of it. Water does not rise two feet and vanish inside one sample, and taking the raw maximum would have posted a record flood at a corner whose real record is 3.1 inches. Requiring the level to hold removes that class of error without touching genuine floods, whose depth by definition persists.

Stuck sensors are removed, and this matters too. A second fault mode looks like the opposite: depth appears instantly, holds one value within a couple of percent for hours, then vanishes. This page flags a reading as a fault when at least 90 percent of its above-floor readings sit within 10 percent of the peak and the coefficient of variation is at most 0.05. Both conditions must hold, and the test runs on every series long enough to count as a flood, so a fault cannot slip through by shrinking as its readings age out of the window. The margin is wide: on a night with genuine flooding, flagged traces ran 100 percent pinned with variation of 0.01 to 0.02, while real floods ran 4 to 35 percent pinned with variation of 0.09 to 0.63. A genuine flood at Utopia Parkway that night traced a clean rise-and-drain curve from 3 inches to 29 and back over 50 minutes, and passed untouched. Flagged sensors are drawn in amber on the map, counted in the readout above and excluded from every number and comparison. Without these two filters the page would have led with a 39-inch flood that never happened. They tighten the comparison rather than bending it, because the historical baseline is built from FloodNet's detected events, which already clear their own quality pipeline.

Why comparisons are per sensor. Sensors sit where FloodNet chose to put them, hot spots first. So a corner with a deep reading is not necessarily worse than a corner without a sensor, and the citywide network of 2026 is not the network of 2022. Every comparison on this page therefore holds location fixed: today's depth at a corner against that same corner's own record, same instrument, same processing.

The citywide count is cohort-locked. The rarity strip ranks today against the past 365 days using only the fixed cohort of sensors that were already installed before the window opened. Every day in that comparison had the chance to hear from the same sensors, so a rise cannot be an artifact of the network growing. Sensors installed more recently appear on the map and in the gauges but are excluded from the ranking.

Seams and limits. The historical dataset lags roughly a week, so the baseline runs through its last published date and today comes from the live feed. A dry map means the sensors are dry, not that no street in the city holds water. Offline sensors are drawn hollow; their silence is not evidence of dry pavement. The API is an early-access beta and field names can change. Depth records begin in November 2020, so "deepest on record" can mean a record only a few years or a few months long, and each gauge states its own start date.

Two feed quirks that change the numbers. The API returns at most 10,000 rows per query, and a wet night across more than 400 sensors produces several times that in 24 hours. A single capped query quietly returns only the most recent slice: while building this page it covered 8 hours while presenting itself as 24, which understated the citywide count by roughly a factor of three. Every window is therefore split in half and refetched until each slice fits under the cap, so coverage is complete rather than silently partial. Separately, a few sensors have faulty clocks and stamp readings years into the future; three were observed reporting dates in 2080. Every query is bounded at both ends so those rows cannot crowd out real data or pose as a location's current reading.

The join was audited. Live sensors carry a different identifier from the historical dataset, so the two are matched on normalized sensor name. Of 416 active sensors, 252 match a flood history and 164 do not. Every one of those 164 was checked against the sensor metadata and found present: they are sensors that have simply never recorded a detected flood, not failed matches. Zero names failed to resolve. Those gauges are labeled "no prior flood" rather than given a baseline they do not have.

Confidence. High for the readings and for the per-sensor comparisons. Medium for the citywide rarity ranking, which depends on the matched-floor assumption above; it is the page's own construction, stated here and visible in build_history.py.
